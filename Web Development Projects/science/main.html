What is Tuberculosis?

Tuberculosis (TB) is a disease caused by the bacterium Mycobacterium tuberculosis. It primarily affects the lungs but can impact other parts of the body. TB can be classified into two types: Latent TB, where the infection is dormant, and Active TB, where the disease is contagious and symptomatic. Symptoms include persistent cough, fever, and weight loss.

Impact on the Body

TB primarily affects the lungs, damaging tissue and reducing oxygen exchange. It can also spread to the lymph nodes, bones, kidneys, and brain, causing inflammation, tissue destruction, and impaired function. Specialized immune cells like macrophages engulf the bacteria, and T-cells coordinate the immune response.

Diagnosis and Treatment

TB is diagnosed using skin tests, blood tests, chest X-rays, or sputum tests. Treatment involves taking antibiotics for 6-9 months, with programs like Directly Observed Therapy (DOT) ensuring adherence. Risks include side effects like nausea or drug resistance from incomplete treatment. In severe cases, surgery may be required to remove damaged tissue.

Prevention and Control

Preventing TB involves vaccination with the BCG vaccine, improving ventilation in crowded spaces, and avoiding close contact with infected individuals. Early testing and treatment for latent TB are also crucial measures to reduce the risk of active disease.

Global Context

Tuberculosis is a global health issue, affecting over 10 million people annually and causing 1.6 million deaths. Certain populations, such as those in low-income regions or with weakened immune systems, are most at risk. Efforts to combat TB include improving healthcare access, raising awareness, and addressing socioeconomic factors contributing to its spread.
