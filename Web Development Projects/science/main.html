What is Tuberculosis?

Tuberculosis is a lung infection that kills millions of people worldwide. It is caused by the microscopic bacteria Mycobacterium tuberculosis. TB spreads through droplets in the air when an infected person coughs, sneezes, or talks. It is possible and common to carry TB without knowing it, a condition called latent TB. According to the United Nations health report, a quarter of the world's population is estimated to carry latent tuberculosis.

TB affects not only the lungs but can also spread to other parts of the body, such as the spine, kidneys, and brain, in what is known as extrapulmonary TB. It remains a global health challenge due to its ability to evade the immune system and develop resistance to antibiotics in some cases.

Fun Fact

Tuberculosis has been around for thousands of years! Traces of TB have been found in Egyptian mummies, indicating that the disease has plagued humanity since ancient times.

Statistical Info

  • TB is one of the top 10 causes of death worldwide, killing an estimated 1.6 million people in 2021.
  • Approximately 10.6 million people fell ill with TB globally in 2021.
  • The Bacillus Calmette-Guérin (BCG) vaccine, introduced in 1921, has saved millions of lives, especially in children.

How Does It Work?

Tuberculosis works by invading the lungs, where the bacteria Mycobacterium tuberculosis begin to multiply. The immune system responds by sending white blood cells to contain the infection, forming small clumps called granulomas. These granulomas can successfully keep the bacteria inactive, leading to latent TB. However, if the immune system weakens—due to malnutrition, stress, or diseases like HIV—the bacteria can break out of these granulomas, causing active TB.

Active TB damages lung tissue, resulting in symptoms such as persistent coughing, chest pain, fatigue, fever, and weight loss. In severe cases, the bacteria can travel through the bloodstream or lymphatic system, infecting other organs and causing conditions like TB meningitis (brain) or TB arthritis (joints). This advanced stage of TB requires urgent medical treatment to prevent life-threatening complications.

Fun Fact

TB bacteria are incredibly tough—they can survive for weeks in the environment outside the human body, waiting to find a new host!

Statistical Info

  • Roughly 450,000 new cases of multi-drug-resistant TB (MDR-TB) are reported annually.
  • People with untreated active TB can infect up to 10-15 others in a year through close contact.
  • In 2021, 1.2 million children developed TB, highlighting its impact on vulnerable populations.
